Inspiring women in science

Biographies

Below are some biographies of inspiring women in science throughout history.

MARY ANNING

1799-1847

Mary Anning was born in Lyme Regis, a popular seaside resort in England. Her father was a carpenter and the family was very poor. Her formal education was very limited although she learned to read and write through her Sunday School.

As an adult Mary continued to collect and sell fossils, but struggled financially for most of her life. Collecting fossils was hard and dangerous work, and she had a near miss with a landslide in 1833 resulting in the death of her dog. In 1823 she found the first complete Plesiosaurus, and in 1828 the first British example of pterosaurs (then called a 'flying dragon' when displayed at the British Museum). She was meticulous in identifying, labelling and classifying her finds.

As a woman she was not able to join the Geological Society of London, and did not always receive credit for her scientific contributions. The only scientific piece of writing that was published in her lifetime was a letter that she wrote to the Magazine of Natural History's editor in 1839. However her discoveries were extremely important, contributing to changes in scientific thinking about prehistoric life and the history of the earth.

ADA LOVELACE

1815 - 1852

Ada Lovelace was born on December 10th in 1815 in England. Educated privately by tutors and self educated, she was helped to advance in her studies by Augustus De Morgan, the frist professor of mathematics at the University of London. In 1833 she was introduced to Charles Babbage, an English mathematician and inventor who is credited with having conceived the first automatic digital computer, The Engine. Ada, was then asked to translate and annotate an article written by the Italian mathematician and engineer Luigi Federico Menabrea, her detailed and elaborate annotations were considered to be excellent; “the Analytical Engine, weaves algebraic patterns, just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves.”

In the end Babbage only built a small part of the Analytical Engine, but Ada had been remembered for her valient efforts to this day. The early programming language Ada was named for her, and the second Tuesday in October has become Ada Lovelace Day, on which the contributions of women to science, technology, engineering, and mathematics are honoured. Today, Ada Lovelace is considered as "The mother of computing"!.

MARY ELLIOTT HILL

1907 - 1969

Mary Elliott Hill was born in 1907 and is one of the first African American women to be awarded with a masters degree in chemistry. She attended Virginia State College in 1925, gaining her degree in 1929. In 1939 she began teaching at Virgina State Unversity's Laboratory Hugh School.

Mary Elliott Hill was an organic and analytic chemist. Her work specified in ultraviolet light and using that to develop analytic methodology. In collaboration with her husband Carl McClellan Hill developed ketene synthesis which was instrumental in the development of plastics.

Mary instituted student chapters of the American Chemical Society at some of the historically black colleges and universities where she taught. It is estimated that at least 20 of her students went on to become chemistry professors, and she won awards for her teaching, demonstrating the power of her influence through education as well as through her immediate contributions to science.

MARIE MAYNARD DALY

1921 - 2003

Dr. Daly was an American biochemist, and the first African-American woman in the United States to receive a PhD in chemistry.

From a young age Marie always had an interest in science. She was inspired by her father's love of science. She studied at Hunter College High School, an all-female institution, where her ambition to become a chemist was encouraged. She enrolled in Queens College and graduated in 1942 with a bachelor's degree in chemistry. She received her doctoral agree in 1947, only three years after enrolling in the program.

In her career she conducted groundbreaking studies on cholesterol, sugars and proteins. Her early research included studies of the effects of cholesterol on the mechanics of the heart, the effects of sugars and other nutrients of the heart of arteries, and the breakdown of the circulatory system as a result of advanced age or hypertension. Dr. Daly went on to study how proteins are produced and organised in the cell.

KATHERINE G. JOHNSON

1918 - 2020

Katherine Johnson was an amazing American mathemetician whose calculations of orbital mathematics as a NASA employee were critical to the success of the first and subsequent U.S crewed spaceflights.

Katherine was born in 1918. Although she had strong mathematical abilities, the County she lived in did not offer public schooling for African-Amercian students beyond 8th grade. Her parents invested in her education by arranging for her to attend school in West Virginia and at just 15, Katherine enrolled in West Virginia State University. In 1937 Katherine graduated with the highest possible honors and then focused on teaching mathematics and West Virginia State's graduate programme, where she was the first ever African American woman in this program.

Katherine began her career as a mathematician at the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics in 1953. She worked with other women, constantly performing calculations and reading data. Her calculations were critical to the success of the first and subsequent US crewed space flights. She was known as a 'human computer' because of her extraordinary mathematical capability and ability to work with space trajectories.

In 2016 a film "Hidden Figures" was released focused on her and her colleagues (two other amazing women): Mary Jackson and Dorothy Vaugn. She was also awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom by then President of the US Barack Obama.

MAE C. JEMISON

1956 -

Mae C. Jemison has a Doctorate in Medicine, is a Chemical Engineer, and was a NASA Astronaut.

In high school, she became interested in biomedical engineering and graduated at the age of 16. She studied a wide range of subjects in lots of locations, from chemical engineering to international medicine in both the US and Kenya. She graduated from medical school in 1981.

She became a medical officer with the Peace Corps in West Africa. While there she worked in conjunction with the National Institutes of Health and the Centers for Disease Control on several research projects, including development of a hepatitis B vaccine.

On her return to the USA, she applied to the National Aeronautics and Space Administration to be an Astronaut. In 1987 she was one of 15 accepted out of 2,000 applicants. Jemison completed her training as a mission specialist with NASA in 1988. On 12 September,1992, Dr. Jemison was the first African American woman to travel in space aboard the Endeavour. She was inspired to become an astronaut because of Star Trek and later appeared on Star Trek where she played Lieutenant Palmer.

“I was excited about the world around me… when you talk about science, it's hard for me to tell you as a child, what drew me to it. I think as an adult looking back, it was the creativity. Understanding what was going on in the world around me.”

Marie Curie

Marie Curie was born in Warsaw on November 7, 1867, to a family of teachers who believed strongly in education. She received a general education in local schools and some scientific training from her father. In 1891, she went to Paris to continue her studies at the Sorbonne where she obtained Licenciateships in Physics and the Mathematical Sciences.

While in Paris she met Pierre Curie, a Professor in the School of Physics, who she married in 1894. Together they discovered radium and polonium. Pierre Curie initially rejected the 1903 Nobel Prize in Physics, only accepting it eventually on the condition that his wife's contribution was also recognised. This made Marie the first-ever female Nobel Laureate. She succeeded her husband as Head of the Physics Laboratory at the Sorbonne, and gained her Doctor of Science degree in 1903.

Marie Curie actively promoted the use of radium to alleviate suffering. During World War I, assisted by her daughter, Irene, she personally devoted herself to this. Her work is recorded in numerous papers in scientific journals and she is the author of 'Recherches sur les Substances Radioactives' (1904), L’Isotopie et les Éléments Isotopes' and the classic 'Traité de Radioactivité' (1910).
